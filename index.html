Question

How has life expectancy changed between 1900 and 2000? We cannot exactly measure this from the data given, but we can approximate it by studying the age distribution in each time period. My sketches attempt to do this in three different ways.

Phase One: Sketching Visualizations

Design Rationale for Sketch 1:

  • Idea behind this sketch was to nod to traditional population pyramid designs used in medicine, but intentionally make it an asymmetric pyramid
  • This Design normalizes out sex differences (by lumping them together) and scale differences (by using percentages, not counts) to focus on distributional shape
  • (Pro) The main advantage of this is it translates the statistical shapes of the populations into a physical shape, and visually contrasts them; you don't need to look at the numbers to see the difference
  • (Pro) Naturally supports visual representations of summary statistics, like median markers for each year
  • (Con) Loses some information about the scales of the populations; this information is still somewhat related to the question
  • (Con) Might be confusing at first because it is a nontraditional use of a population pyramid

Design Rationale for Sketch 2:

  • Previous sketch ignored scale; this vis very intentionally does not. Number of dots corresponds to population counts, hence many additional dots in 2000
  • Uses a coarser age grouping (child, adult, elderly) because using all 5-year age groups would result in visual overload
  • Still normalizes out sex information because it is not very relevant to the question
  • (Pro) Communicates distributional information using color and pattern/repetition; again, reader would not need to look at numbers to get the point
  • (Pro) Coarser age grouping is much simpler to understand
  • (Pro) Visualizes the scale of the populations
  • (Con) Normalizes out some important information, e.g. it wouldn't show the apparent bi-modal shape of the 2000 census data
  • (Con) A little harder to visually represent summary stats

Design Rationale for Sketch 3:

  • Both previous plots ignored sex information and separately visualized each year. This sketch attempts to do both of those things differently
  • Focuses specifically on the change in each age group over the century, with the goal of showing that concentration has shifted heavily toward an older/adult + elderly population
  • Bar chart format naturally lends itself to stacking, so we can see changes in male and female subpopulations
  • The focal point would be toward the end of the plot, where the increase in elderly population would be quite sharp
  • (Pro) Bar charts are easy to parse, and there is low risk for visual overload here
  • (Con) Deltas are confusing, especially in this stacked format; e.g., requires a lot of reflective processing to determine whether male or female changed more
  • (Con) Would not easily support case where, e.g., percent of men increased but percent of women decreased, though I think this is unlikely

Reflection

All three of my visualizations look quite different visually, and seem to tackle different aspects of my original question. The first visualization focuses on distributions, and really emphasizes shape in the visual design. The second incorporates scale and is pleasantly simple to look at. I also appreciate that this simplifies the age groupings into something that is easy to parse, but I worry that this will lose too much of the shape information that Sketch 1 captures. Finally, the third sketch really leans into the idea of change. While it's more detailed than the other plots, I think it's a bit too confusing. As I consider the first and second sketches together, I am drawn to the first because of how it draws from a very conventional and recognizable design, but changes it in an interesting way. Using shape as the key visual element feels like it resonates with the question I am asking, which is fundamentally about shape. To take it further, I think I would need to refine this design a bit: using density (area under curve) makes it harder to see the individual age groups, and there is room to show additional summary statistics on the plot.

Phase Two: Final Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

My goal with this visualization was to communicate the dramatic change in shape of the US population between 1900 and 2000, highlighting that people live much longer now than they did 100 years ago. To accomplish this, I chose a design that made shape the focal point of the visualization. The pyramidal shape for 1900 visually encodes the dropoff in population size with age, while the twin peaks in 2000 show that the population is now concentrated in the middle-aged and elderly groups. To doubly emphasize the difference in average age, I lightly hatched the median age groups for each year, including a small legend to indicate what this means.

Unlike traditional population pyramids, I chose to put the years next to each other rather than the sexes because I wanted these shape differences to sit directly next to each other, and sex did not seem relevant to the question I was asking. I arranged the pyramids with 1900 on the left and 2000 on the right to reflect their chronological order, and chose a burnt sienna color for 1900 to give it a more historical feel, and chose a futuristic-feeling green for 2000 to contrast with it. These colors pair well together on their own, but I wanted them to nod to the time periods they represent.

I chose to keep all the age groups separate, rather than grouping them into higher-level categories, to most fully capture the shape of each population. I additionally normalized the population counts to use percentages rather than raw counts for the same reason; had I kept the scale, I think it would have distracted from the shape element and drawn the focus more toward the different sizes of each population. Using percentages allowed me to rescale the x-axis to the same size on both sides of the plot and use only a couple of tick marks, which denoised the plot.
